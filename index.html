Cross-Asset Contagion: The 2026 Iran Conflict Oil Shock

Overview

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched joint military strikes against Iran in what the U.S. codenamed Operation Epic Fury. The strikes targeted Iranian leadership and military infrastructure, triggering immediate retaliation and disrupting traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, a critical chokepoint through which approximately 20% of global oil supply transits daily.

This project examines how the resulting oil supply shock propagated across global asset classes, measuring the speed, magnitude, and direction of contagion from commodities into equities, volatility, interest rates, and currencies. The analysis uses 14 instruments tracked over a 3-month window (December 1, 2025 – March 9, 2026), with February 28 as the event date dividing the pre-conflict baseline from the crisis period.

Methodology

The analysis is built on an Excel model feeding into a four-panel Tableau dashboard. The data pipeline follows a structured approach:

Data collection: Daily closing prices for 14 instruments sourced from the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis (FRED) and Investing.com, covering commodities (Brent Crude, WTI Crude, Natural Gas), equities (S&P 500, XLE Energy ETF, ITA Defense ETF, JETS Airlines ETF), volatility (VIX), interest rates (10-Year Treasury Yield, 10-Year Breakeven Inflation), and currencies (USD Broad Index, USD/JPY, USD/INR, USD/THB).

Excel model: The workbook contains seven tabs: Raw Data, Daily Returns (live percentage-change formulas), Normalized Prices (rebased to 100 on February 27, the last pre-conflict trading day), Correlation Analysis (pre-conflict vs. during-conflict correlation matrices), Event Timeline, Summary Statistics, and Data Sources. All calculations use live Excel formulas, allowing the model to be updated as new data becomes available.

Tableau dashboard: Four visualizations assembled into a single interactive dashboard: a normalized price line chart (the primary view), a cumulative return bar chart (winners vs. losers), a volatility comparison (pre-conflict vs. during-conflict), and a correlation shift chart (how each asset’s relationship with Brent Crude changed when the crisis hit).

Key Findings

1. The Oil Supply Shock

Brent Crude surged 32.3% from $71.32 on February 27 to $94.35 by March 9, while WTI Crude rose even more sharply at 41.4%. The disruption to the Strait of Hormuz effectively halted approximately 20 million barrels per day of transit, creating an immediate supply shortfall. Despite OPEC+ announcing a 206,000-barrel/day output increase and the IEA coordinating its largest-ever Strategic Petroleum Reserve release (172 million barrels from the U.S. alone), oil prices remained elevated throughout the period.

A notable finding was that the ITA Defense ETF slightly declined (-0.7%) despite the prevailing narrative that defense stocks benefit from military conflict. This may reflect broader equity selling pressure overwhelming sector-specific tailwinds, or the market’s expectation that the conflict would be short-lived.

3. Volatility Explosion

Daily volatility (measured as the standard deviation of daily returns) increased across nearly every asset class during the conflict. The most dramatic shifts were in the VIX, where daily volatility rose from 6.8% to 16.0% (a 136% increase), and in Brent Crude, where volatility tripled from 1.9% to 5.5%. Even traditionally stable instruments like the USD Broad Index saw volatility nearly double from 0.2% to 0.4%. The only asset where volatility declined was Natural Gas, which had already experienced extreme volatility in January due to Winter Storm Fern (discussed separately below).

4. Correlation Regime Change

The most analytically significant finding was the dramatic shift in correlation structures. During the pre-conflict period, most assets had weak or near-zero correlation with Brent Crude. When the crisis hit, these relationships transformed:

  • S&P 500: Correlation with Brent shifted from +0.01 to -0.95. This near-perfect negative correlation means that as oil spiked, equities fell almost in lockstep — a textbook contagion pattern where a commodity shock translates into equity risk.
  • VIX: Moved from +0.07 to +0.93. Pre-conflict, VIX was essentially uncorrelated with oil. During the crisis, fear moved in near-perfect tandem with oil prices; as oil spiked, so did market volatility.
  • USD/THB: Shifted from -0.08 to +0.81. Thailand is heavily dependent on Gulf oil imports. As oil prices surged, the Thai Baht weakened against the dollar, reflecting the expected current account deterioration for oil-importing emerging markets.
  • USD Broad Index: Moved from -0.16 to +0.69. The dollar strengthened alongside oil, likely reflecting safe-haven flows into the U.S. currency during geopolitical uncertainty.
  • JETS (Airlines): Moved from -0.21 to -0.80. The already-negative relationship deepened significantly, as higher oil prices directly increase airlines’ highest operating cost.

This correlation regime change is a critical insight for risk management. During normal times, portfolio diversification across these asset classes provides genuine risk reduction. During a geopolitical crisis, however, correlations converge — assets that were previously uncorrelated begin moving together, reducing the effectiveness of diversification precisely when it is needed most. This is the core mechanism of financial contagion.

Separate Note: The January Natural Gas Spike

Natural Gas was excluded from the charts due to an extreme spike in January 2026 that distorted the Y-axis, compressing all other assets into an unreadable band. This spike was driven by Winter Storm Fern, not the Iran conflict. Henry Hub prices surged approximately 170% in a single week in late January, jumping from $3.04/MMBtu to $8.21/MMBtu, with some regional hubs recording all-time highs exceeding $30/MMBtu. The storm set 79 all-time high average spot price records across the country, surpassing even Winter Storm Uri’s 2021 tally of 77 records.

This is analytically significant for two reasons. First, it demonstrates that Natural Gas was already in a heightened volatility regime before the Iran conflict began, which is why its during-conflict volatility actually decreased relative to its pre-conflict period (the January storm inflated the baseline). Second, it illustrates why risk dashboards must identify and separate distinct risk events rather than conflating them. The natural gas spike was a weather-driven domestic supply shock, fundamentally different from the geopolitical oil supply shock that followed a month later.
