Cross-Asset Contagion: The 2026 Iran Conflict Oil Shock

How the Feb 28 US-Israel military strikes on Iran rippled across 14 asset classes — from oil to equities, volatility, rates, and emerging market currencies.

Overview

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched joint military strikes against Iran, codenamed Operation Epic Fury. The strikes targeted Iranian leadership and military infrastructure, triggering immediate retaliation and disrupting traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — a critical chokepoint through which approximately 20% of global oil supply transits daily.

This project examines how the resulting oil supply shock propagated across global asset classes, measuring the speed, magnitude, and direction of contagion from commodities into equities, volatility, interest rates, and currencies. The analysis uses 14 instruments tracked over 71 trading days (December 1, 2025 – March 9, 2026), with February 28 as the event date.

Methodology

The analysis is built on a 7-tab Excel model feeding into a 4-panel Tableau dashboard. The Excel workbook contains: Raw Data, Daily Returns (live percentage-change formulas), Normalized Prices (rebased to 100 on Feb 27), Correlation Analysis (pre-conflict vs. during-conflict matrices), Event Timeline, Summary Statistics, and Data Sources. All calculations use live Excel formulas, allowing the model to update as new data becomes available.

Data was sourced from FRED (Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis) for commodities, volatility, rates, and major currencies, and from Investing.com for equity ETFs and emerging market exchange rates.

Key Findings

1. The Oil Supply Shock

Brent Crude surged 32.3% from $71.32 to $94.35. WTI rose 41.4%. Despite OPEC+ output increases and the IEA's largest-ever SPR release (172M barrels from the US), prices remained elevated throughout the period.

2. Clear Winners and Losers

Oil and VIX (+28.4%) surged. Airlines (JETS) fell -9.4% as fuel costs spiked. Notably, the ITA Defense ETF slightly declined -0.7% despite the war narrative — broader equity selling pressure may have overwhelmed sector tailwinds.

3. Volatility Explosion

Daily volatility increased across nearly every asset. VIX daily vol rose from 6.8% to 16.0% (+136%). Brent volatility tripled from 1.9% to 5.5%. Even the USD Broad Index saw volatility nearly double.

4. Correlation Regime Change

The most significant finding: S&P 500 correlation with Brent shifted from +0.01 to -0.95. VIX moved from +0.07 to +0.93. Diversification failed precisely when it was needed most — the textbook definition of contagion.

Natural Gas: A Separate Risk Event

Why Natural Gas was excluded from the main chart: In January 2026, Winter Storm Fern caused Henry Hub prices to spike ~170% in a single week ($3.04 to $8.21/MMBtu), setting 79 all-time spot price records. This weather-driven domestic supply shock was fundamentally different from the geopolitical oil shock that followed a month later. Including it would have compressed all other assets into an unreadable band. This separation illustrates why risk dashboards must identify and isolate distinct risk events rather than conflating them.
